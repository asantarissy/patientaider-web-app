Drug Errors

A drug error is when a patient gets the wrong drug, the wrong drug amount, the drug the wrong way, or gets the drug at the wrong time.

About 1.5 million preventable drug errors occur each year in US hospitals.4

About 4,110 patients a day!

Any of these symptoms can be from a drug error:5

  • Falling asleep mid-sentence
  • Confusion
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Breathing too slowly
  • Itching, rash or hives
  • Partial or total paralysis
  • Severe pain
  • Headache
  • Stomach ache
  • Seizure
  • Loss of muscle control
  • Difficulty speaking
  • Increased or decreased heart rate
  • Vision loss
  • Nausea, constipation or diarrhea
  • Dizziness
  • Swelling of lips, tongue or face
  • Low or high blood pressure

Normal adult breathing rate is 12 to 16 breaths per minute. You can count how many times the patient's chest rises over 1 minute. 23

Drug Error Prevention

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/9jmULQ_m04o

1. Bring the patient's prescription and over-the-counter medications, vitamins, supplements and allergy list to show the doctor and nurse.1

Taking Medications-What to Ask Your Doctor from MedlinePlus

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/MWUM7LIXDeA

2. Bring a drug manual, use a smartphone app, or any of these websites for drug information and interactions.1

Drug Reaction Information from the National Library of Medicine

Drug Reaction Information from MedlinePlus

Drugs, Herbs and Supplement Information from MedlinePlus

3. Ask for a copy of your or the patient's Medication Administration Record or MAR from your doctor or nurse.1

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/BJI1ToB-Dv8

5 Questions to Ask About Your Medications from ISMP Canada

5 Moments for Medication Safety from the World Health Organization

4. Make sure your nurse scans your wristband to verify your name before giving you any medication if the hospital has this system.1

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/rbVO5Qc_wJ8

5. Ask if the pills look different from your medications at home.1

Opioids After Surgery from ISMP Canada

6. Ask questions if you aren’t sure why your nurse is giving you a drug.1

Medication Error Prevention from the Institute for Safe Medication Practices

7. Share your hospital drug list with your family and/or advocate when changes are made.1

Your Medicine: Be Smart. Be Safe from AHRQ

8. Ask for a hospital pharmacist to visit if your questions aren’t explained to you or the patient.1

9. Don’t take supplements or medications without telling your nurse and doctor.1

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/wIbfwlV1XH4

Opioid Pain Medication Information from ISMP Canada

10. Constipation is common side effect from opioid (narcotic) medications-the patient should get bowel medications to prevent this.1

Hospital Medication Safety Information by the National Library of Medicine Opioid Fact Sheet from the Empowered Patient Coalition

11. Report all drug reactions to your nurse at the hospital. Tell your nurse to report any drug error. Ask for the nursing supervisor or attending doctor regarding this adverse drug report.1

You or your advocate can report a drug error to: Institute for Safe Medication Practices Medication Errors Reporting Program (MERP): 1-215-947-77977 or: Institute for Safe Medication Practices Drug Error Report
