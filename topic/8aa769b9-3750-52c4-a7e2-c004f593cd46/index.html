Infections

About 75,000 patients die every year from healthcare-associated infections.

That's enough people to fill 2 baseball stadiums!

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/-FfMCv8FUXI

Common healthcare-associated infections are:1

  • Catheter-related Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs)
  • Clostridium difficile Infections (C.diff)
  • Surgical Site Infections
  • Hospital-acquired Pneumonia
  • Central Line-associated Bloodstream Infections

Any of the following can be caused by an infection:23

  • Extreme pain
  • Fever or chills
  • Redness, swelling, &/or pus
  • Confusion
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Very slow breathing
  • Dizziness
  • Unusual sweating
  • Rash or hives
  • Drop in blood pressure
  • High or low temperature
  • Changes in heart rate
  • Changes in breathing
  • Decrease in urine
  • The patient doesn’t look right

Call the nurse if the patient “doesn’t seem right” or if the alarm goes off.23

Listen to your gut or intuition. If no one is listening to you, speak with the Nurse Manager.

Infection Prevention

All healthcare providers and visitors must wash their hands. The average handwashing rate is 40% for healthcare providers in the hospital.4 Never be afraid to ask your doctor or nurse to please wash their hands!

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/D_vRYhEKAKA

Clean Hands Count from the CDC

Embedded content: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l4HpcOpQaoQ

1. Bring in hand sanitizer for the patient, healthcare providers, and visitors to use. Ask your doctor or nurse to wash their hands with you.

2. Use disinfectant wipes on all surfaces that you or the patient will come in contact with. Ask the nurse for these.5

3. Put up a sign telling staff to wash their hands on your door and above your bed.5

4. Make sure doctors and nurses wipe the round end of the stethoscope with an alcohol wipe.5

Embedded content: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M8AKTACyiB0

5. Gloves need to be worn when there is contact with blood or other body fluids, mouth, nose, lungs, urinary and digestive tracts, open skin areas, or items that are soiled with blood or other body fluids. Healthcare workers need to wash their hands both before and after using gloves. Wearing gloves is never a substitute for hand washing!2 Gloves can have microscopic tears and holes.

6. Healthcare providers and visitors need to follow Isolation Precautions of wearing gowns, gloves and sometimes masks if the patient's door has an Isolation Precaution sign on it.

Isolation Precaution Information from the National Library of Medicine

7. The patient must wash their hands before eating every meal and before going to sleep.2

8. Cellphones are dirty67

  • Wipe your cell phone with an alcohol wipe.
  • Do not take a cell phone into the bathroom.
  • Do not place the cell phone on the floor, especially the bathroom floor.

9. Cover your mouth when you cough or sneeze. Use your elbow or arm instead of your hands.

10. When a nurse is giving IV medications, make sure the nurse cleans the IV port for 15 seconds before and after with an alcohol wipe.8 Don’t touch where your IV lines meet your skin.9

11. Tell your nurse and doctor if there is pain, swelling, redness, itching or wetness at an IV line or incision.

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/67vDXQMxUz4

12. Ask if you or the patient can have their own blood pressure cuff to be used only by them.2

13. Remove your slippers worn over socks before getting back into your bed. This prevents germs from the floor coming into your bed.2

14. All visitors need to:2

  • Wash their hands before entering and leaving the room
  • Do not visit if they feel ill
  • Avoid using the patient’s bathroom

2. Urinary catheters should be removed within 2 days of being inserted. If a urinary catheter needs to be inserted, it needs to be done under sterile conditions.2

Prevention of Clostridium difficile (C. difficile) Infection

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/E3AspGb-QA4

Healthcare providers and visitors must wash their hands with soap and water and wear gloves, gowns, and/or masks (Isolation Precautions) when caring for patients who have a C. difficile infection.

Soap and water are more effective at hand cleaning than hand sanitizer with a C. difficile infection.10

Prevention of Surgical Site Infection

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/gaBJUR-8QvE

Before Surgery:

To prevent surgical site infections the CDC infection prevention guidelines are:

  • Doctors and nurses need to clean their hands with soap and water or an alcohol-based hand rub before and after caring for each patient.
  • In most cases, you should get antibiotics within 60 minutes before surgery starts and the antibiotics should be stopped within 24 hours after surgery.
  • Clean the patient's skin at the site of surgery with a special soap that kills germs before surgery.

After surgery:

  • If you do not see your providers clean their hands, please ask them to do so.
  • Family and friends who visit you should not touch the surgical wound or dressings.
  • Family and friends should clean their hands with soap and water or an alcohol-based hand rub before and after visiting you. If you do not see them clean their hands, ask them to clean their hands.
  • Make sure you understand how to care for your wound before you leave the hospital.
  • Always clean your hands before and after caring for your wound.
  • Make sure you know who to contact if you have questions or problems after you get home.
  • If you have any symptoms of an infection, such as redness and pain at the surgery site, drainage, or fever in the hospital, call your RN.11

Prevention of Hospital-acquired Pneumonia

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/4rFjAmaDXlg

If the patient is on a ventilator1213:

  • Ask for a daily check of the patient's ability to breathe without the ventilator's help.
  • Help make sure the patient's back and head are always at least at a 30 degree angle.
  • Tell the nurse if the patient slides down in the bed.
  • Ask about reducing the amount of sedation medication or a "sedation vacation".
  • Ask about walking the patient.
  • Watch for bed sores on the patient's lower back and buttocks.
  • Encourage deep breathing with the incentive spirometer. 
  • The patient should do at least 10 deep breaths every hour using the incentive spirometer while in bed.14

Prevention of Central Catheter Intravenous Line Infection

Embedded content: https://youtu.be/sFROH5rvWOY

Don’t touch where your IV lines are.

When a central catheter IV line is inserted, make sure there is:

  • Careful hand washing
  • Barrier precautions used during line insertion (including hair covers, masks, sterile gloves, sterile gown, eye protection)
  • Cleaning the skin with chlorhexidine gluconate skin prep
  • Avoid using the femoral vein (upper leg near the groin)
  • Daily review of the need for the central catheter IV and prompt removal of unnecessary IVs.1516
